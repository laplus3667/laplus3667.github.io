Proof of Proposition 1.

  1. First, recall that the incumbent price $\hat{p}$ satisfies $\rho_{0}(\hat{p})=\rho_{1}(\hat{p})$.
  2. Now, let $\tilde{p}$ be an arbitrary price in $\left[p_{0}^{*}, p_{1}^{*}\right]$ such that $\rho_{1}(\tilde{p})=\rho_{0}(\tilde{p})$. Because $\varepsilon_{1}(p)<1<$ $\varepsilon_{0}(p)$ for all $p \in\left[p_{0}^{*}, p_{1}^{*}\right]$, we deduce that $\rho_{1}^{\prime}(\tilde{p})-\rho_{0}^{\prime}(\tilde{p})>0$.
  3. Thus, the function $p \mapsto \rho_{1}(p)-\rho_{0}(p)$ is locally increasing around $\tilde{p}$ and vanishes at that point.
  4. This implies that there can be at most one price $p \in\left[p_{0}^{*}, p_{1}^{*}\right]$ satisfying $\rho_{1}(p)=\rho_{0}(p)$.
  5. Because the incumbent price $\hat{p}$ already satisfies that condition, we conclude that $\hat{p}$ is the unique price $p \in\left[p_{0}^{*}, p_{1}^{*}\right]$ such that $\rho_{1}(p)=\rho_{0}(p)$.
